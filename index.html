Challenge
Three linked briefs on Databricks/Apache Spark: predict churn and stream customer events at scale; audit whether a support operation's data can be trusted; and map a real brand's social media network and sentiment.
My role
4-person team (Group 21, Nova IMS). I led the predictive modelling and real-time streaming notebook; all three notebooks were built and reported on collaboratively.
Approach
Built a Random Forest churn model on a 1M-row dataset with a full Spark ML feature pipeline, then a Structured Streaming module with watermarking and windowed aggregation. Separately, audited 200,000 support tickets across 15 analytical cuts (priority, tier, product, channel, region) using Spark SQL, DataFrames and RDDs on a Delta Lake star schema. And built a GraphFrames co-engagement network — PageRank, community detection — plus an ML sentiment classifier on real anonymised social data from Bounce, a luggage-storage brand.
Tools
Databricks, Apache Spark (PySpark), Spark SQL, Spark ML, Spark Structured Streaming, GraphFrames, Delta Lake
Outcome
Churn model: AUC 0.61, with contract length identified as the dominant driver (~90% of feature importance) — a clear, actionable retention lever. Support audit: found that response time (~36 hrs), resolution time (~120 hrs) and CSAT (3.0) were statistically uniform across every priority, product, tier and channel — diagnosed as a data governance failure, not real operational parity, and packaged into a 4-part remediation roadmap. Bounce network analysis: sentiment classifier at 69.5% accuracy / 0.69 F1 across 3,172 cleaned messages; found negative-sentiment users sit inside 121,078 of 182,122 engagement paths (well-connected, high spread risk); delivered 6 platform-specific marketing recommendations.
Why it matters
This is the closest project to production data work: distributed processing that doesn't fall over at scale, and — in the support audit — the judgement to recognise when flat metrics mean the data can't be trusted, not that the business is running smoothly. That distinction is worth more to a hiring manager than a clean model on a tidy dataset.

Challenge
Forecast daily demand to support staffing and marketing-spend decisions; separately, predict which customers will respond to a marketing campaign so budget isn't wasted on the wrong contacts.
My role
5-person team ("Group U", Nova IMS). Both notebooks were built and reported on collaboratively.
Approach
Regression: cleaned and feature-engineered a 731-day bike-rental dataset (weather, calendar, engineered ratio features), then progressed through Linear Regression, Decision Tree, Random Forest and a grid-search-tuned Gradient Boosting model. Classification: cleaned a 2,233-customer campaign dataset, engineered RFM/tenure/spend features, addressed severe class imbalance with SMOTE, and compared Logistic Regression against XGBoost — then tuned the decision threshold around a business trade-off (precision vs. recall) rather than defaulting to 0.5.
Tools
Python, scikit-learn, XGBoost, imbalanced-learn (SMOTE), Yellowbrick, GridSearchCV
Outcome
Demand forecasting: tuned Gradient Boosting reached R\u00b2 0.785 (MAE 709 bikes) on held-out data, with temperature and a wind/weather interaction as the strongest drivers — translated into a staffing buffer and demand-triggered marketing-spend rule. Campaign response: XGBoost (ROC-AUC 0.907) beat a Logistic Regression baseline (ROC-AUC 0.888); at a business-tuned threshold, precision reached 0.71 vs. 0.57 for the baseline — roughly 7 in 10 targeted customers were genuine leads — a deliberate trade against recall to cut wasted campaign spend. Feature importance fed 8 concrete recommendations, including flagging one historically underperforming campaign for discontinuation and defining a "VIP win-back" segment.
Why it matters
Both projects stop at a business decision, not a metric — choosing precision over recall because of what it costs to contact a non-responder is exactly the kind of trade-off a marketing analytics role actually requires.
